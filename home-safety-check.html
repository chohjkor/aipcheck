Is Your Home Ready for Aging in Place?

Many people assume their current home will continue to meet their needs as they age. In reality, everyday living spaces can become increasingly challenging over time.

1. Safe Movement and Accessibility

Stairs, narrow hallways, and uneven surfaces can increase the risk of falls. Clear pathways and accessible entrances are important for long-term safety.

2. Bathroom and Kitchen Safety

Bathrooms and kitchens are common locations for household accidents. Slip-resistant surfaces, adequate space, and thoughtful layouts play a key role in reducing injury risks.

3. Lighting and Visibility

Poor lighting can make navigation difficult and increase accident risks. Proper illumination supports both safety and comfort.

4. Emergency Preparedness

The ability to respond to emergencies, such as sudden illness or accidents, should be considered when evaluating a living environment.

5. Adaptability Over Time

A home that can be modified or adjusted as needs change is better suited for long-term independent living.

These factors vary depending on individual circumstances. AIP Check is designed to support personal awareness and self-assessment, not to provide professional diagnoses or guarantees.

Who should use this checklist

This checklist is designed for individuals who want to evaluate their current home before problems occur. It is especially helpful for people planning long-term independent living without relying on institutional care.
